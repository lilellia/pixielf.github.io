[Y4] Afterimage of a Crimson Eye

Written: February 2018

This, along with all of the poems whose names start with brackets, are taken from my time as one of the writers for a mod of Doki Doki Literature Club a few years ago. In particular, I served as the lead writer for both Sayori's and Monika's routes as well as the lead poem writer (and lead programmer, but that's less relevant). The mod was designed to fit the game more typically into the romance visual novel genre and, as such, give Monika a normal characterization. The other characters' backstories were also fleshed out.

While I ended up writing a total of 19 poems for this project (4 each for Sayori, Monika, Yuri, and the player character and 3 for Natsuki), only some of them are preserved here. These were all an interesting challenge: the four girls would write four poems over the course of the game, and they all write about their own character conflicts in such a way that the later poems are more clear, forward, and candid about their conflicts. As an example, Yuri's first poem, Fading Light, uses the image of her staying up all night reading to rather innocently show her isolationist tendencies, desire to use books as an escape, and accordant loneliness. Afterimage, however, as her fourth poem, showcases her conflict most strongly, and—for this one in particular—serves as a vague manifestation of her backstory. Another fun challenge for this set was that, while the poems themselves were not shown in the original game, the titles of the girls' fourth poems were shown along with a brief description of the poems through narration, which I used to actually create those poems.

あたしって、ほんとバカ・「ウソをついて」
atashi tte, honto baka・"uso wo tsuite"

Written: August 2020

There's... a lot to unpack here. Definitely go read the poem first. I promise, this explanation will work a lot better without spoilers. Done? Cool.

The poem as a whole is designed to do two opposing things simultaneously. On the one hand, a naïve or surface reading sees the poem supporting a positive, optimistic, congratulatory mood and interpretation. On the other hand... well, it's actually the opposite:

あたしって、本当バカ・ウソをついて

I hear their words carry through the air,
Refined and full of grace,
Incapable of being but true.

All these beautiful promises
Are no great show of importance,
But the simple things,
Worth a world unto themselves.

All these beautiful promises
Ring out in confluent octaves,
Washing away my scars
Earned from chasing mirages
Half-revealed by the glint of the moon.

All these beautiful promises
Reach out to me with all their stars' warmth,
Unmatched by the soulless sword of dark
With no power to slice through fate.

All these beautiful promises
Come from a home, your affection,
Reminders of what great kindness may bring forth,
If only we'd believe.

atashi tte, honto baka

These are Miki Sayaka's final words in the anime Puella Magi Madoka Magica, and they also serve as the title of the episode in which they're spoken¹. Based on my knowledge of the scene, as well as my very limited Japanese, I'm not sure I've ever seen a perfectly faithful translation because the depth is hard to encapsulate, but Crunchyroll translates it as "I was stupid... so stupid."

In particular, she was "so stupid" to believe that

  1. having her wish granted would make her happy — It doesn't. In becoming a magical girl, one makes a contract with Kyubey, who grants a wish in return. So, Sayaka trades her soul² and becomes a magical girl in order to cure the boy she has a crush on (who was in an accident and lost use of his arm, making it impossible for the prodigy to play the violin). Despite being unable to tell him the truth, she hopes that it will enable them to date, but he just ends up dating another girl instead. This realization helps to accelerate her realization that what she wants is to be recognized for her actions and that she's not nearly as selfless as she thinks she is.
  2. she'll be able to make a difference as a magical girl — She doesn't. As an idealist³, she conflicts with Homura and Kyoko, and tells them that she will fight for people, not for herself. Combined with the reveal of the dark nature of the powers of the magical girl (see ²), she drives herself way~ too far, pushes her best friend away for casting doubt on her methods (while not being a magical girl herself), refuses help from Homura, and ultimately ends up driving herself to death. By the end (which isn't even that long since becoming a magical girl, she tells Kyouko that she "can't even remember what [she] thought was so important, so worth protecting..."

Don't forget... that kindness can sometimes bring forth an even greater sadness.

Specifically, Homura is alluding to the fact that she's been reliving the past month (thanks to her time magic) close to 100 times for the sole purpose of trying to save Madoka from her fate, which... never ends up working. She sees Madoka as her only real friend, but Homura's the only one who can remember the other timelines. The irony (and falsity) of Madoka's promise to "never forget" Homura hits her hard, breaking her generally distant, aloof appearance.

The overall structure of the poem is set up so that knowing a couple references (which, apart from Google Translating the title, I don't expect anyone to notice) entirely changes the tone: while the poem itself seems a celebration of the small promises and gestures made by our friends, the two Japanese titles, the line All these beautiful promises, and the line Reminders of what such kindness may bring forth are all designed to corrupt this interpretation. The poem's actual point is about how meaningless or even detrimental or painful promises can be since they can (and perhaps even often) come across as hollow or simply are broken. The counterintuitive nature of this claim is also supported by the poem's counterintuitive structure in the truth being hidden.

Mirror

Written: November 2018

This one might be my favorite. Early 2018 was really the first time I was writing any poetry at all, and those DDLC mod poems were a fun challenge between the different styles I had to try to match and the constraints I set myself. But I think Mirror is the first one that I wrote "on my own", and it was the start of me using poetry as a sort of coping strategy. Afterimage aside, most of the poems I write aren't always obnoxiously cryptic, but I definitely prefer having a layer of abstraction between the actual context and what's written on the page, and that stems from the fact that they're mostly focused on/inspired by things which I find difficult to talk about candidly. But, finding an image or metaphor that works to convey what I want, then putting in the effort to write it means that the poem can express things that I wouldn't be able to otherwise, so I've found it helpful as both a "get it off my chest" kind of thing as well as a productive distraction, which often helps pull me out of the worst of the emotional rut.

淋しい
samishii

Written: October 2019

淋しい (samishii) is a Japanese word used to express loneliness or a desire to meet with someone. Since then, I've heard it used in much more innocent contexts, but I originally came across the word on a web page talking about different ways to express "I miss you". There, 淋しい is listed as (paraphrased) "I'm lonely because you're not here, and it's not okay". In particular, there's a choice for the kanji—寂しい vs. 淋しい—and the site listed the second of these as slightly more grave. But, again, I've seen the word used a lot more freely than that, as a much more general "I'm lonely", so make of that what you will.

Sí avisí bý

Written: August 02, 2020

Good luck making sense of this one given that it's not a language you'll find in Google Translate. As mentioned in my Mirror comment, I'm a fan of draping a layer of intrigue over something that I'm too shy/embarrassed/uncomfortable talking about, and languages are one of my favorite strategies for that, usually for a meta "the fact that I'm saying a thing that I know you can't understand is the point because I need to express it without you actually understanding" kind of thing. There's a certain level of that here.

If you want some grammar hints to try to make heads or tails of the poem on your own:

  • arí = 1s nominative, arèl = 2s nominative
  • ékl— = nominative → accusative marker for personal pronouns (i.e., éklarí = 1s acc.)
  • st— = nominative → dative marker for personal pronouns (i.e., starí = 1s dat.)
  • ís X = marks "X" as direct object (though ís + ari = éklarí, etc.)
  • lí Y = marks "Y" as indirect object (though lí + ari = starí, etc.)
  • sí X = attributive marker: regardless of its part of speech (though usu. noun/adj.), "X" functions as an adjective whose attribute is being given to whichever object is relevant
  • bý = "to be" (verbs don't conjugate). Combining this with the previous point, X sí Y bý = "X is [like, of similar to, embodying, etc.] Y."

[S3] Wind

Written: February 2018

If Yuri's conflict was about anxiety and PTSD and struggling with self harm, and Monika's was about finding her own place in the world and gaining control over her life, Sayori's conflict was struggling with suicidal depression. She's the type whom you wouldn't immediately expect to be depressed because she's always the one who's most energetic and excited and will cheer you up in a heartbeat, but it's all a shell. "Favorite" isn't the right word, but the part of the original game I'll always appreciate the most is how Sayori's depression confession is handled. Unlike in most media, the way she talks and thinks about it feels so refreshingly genuine… if extraordinarily painful, and I think that, especially with the game's popularity, it helped people to understand the condition. In fact, when it came for me to write Sayori's depression confession scene, I mostly just adapted the original scene instead.
